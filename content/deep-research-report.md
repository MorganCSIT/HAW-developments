# Business Plan for a Phuket Specialty Physical Therapy and Rehabilitation Center

## Executive Summary

The strongest BOI-led structure for this project is to make the promoted core business a **specialty medical center focused on physical therapy** under BOI activity **2.2.1.1**, and to treat the overnight rehabilitation element as a **supporting, tightly limited adjunct** to intensive rehabilitation episodes rather than as the primary identity of the project. BOI’s current published English guide classifies **physical therapy** as a shortage-area service eligible under **2.2.1.1 Specialty medical center**, with **A2** incentives, while **2.2.2.2 Health rehabilitation center** is a **Group B** activity that requires at least **30 million baht** investment, medical technology for treatment and rehabilitation, and continuous rehabilitation programs including overnight treatment, but does **not** receive a corporate income tax exemption. citeturn3view0turn9view1turn9view2

Commercially, this supports a differentiated concept in Phuket: a **Swiss-standard mobility and restorative recovery center** aimed at post-operative orthopedic recovery, stroke and neurological rehabilitation, geriatric mobility restoration, chronic musculoskeletal pain, and medically supervised performance recovery. Thailand’s aging trend is structurally favorable: the National Statistical Office reports that people aged 60+ accounted for **20.0%** of Thailand’s population in 2024, and province-level NSO data show Phuket with an elderly rate of **16.31%** and an estimated **100,895** older persons. Phuket also remains a very large access market for domestic and foreign patients; the Ministry of Tourism and Sports reported **10.49 million** tourists and travelers to Phuket in 2023, generating **353.0 billion baht** in tourism revenue, while Phuket International Airport continues to serve domestic and international traffic. citeturn18search4turn18search1turn17search0turn37search0

The most important legal and tax nuance is that an A2 project is not simply an “8-year free-tax box.” BOI’s guide states that A2 activities receive **8 years** of CIT exemption, but for **A2–A4** activities the exemption is generally **capped at 100% of project investment as determined by BOI**. Thailand’s standard corporate income tax rate is **20%** on net profit. That means the headline value of the tax holiday depends heavily on the BOI-recognized promoted investment base and on how cleanly the company separates promoted physical-therapy income from non-promoted lodging, hospitality, and wellness income. citeturn28view0turn20search0

## BOI Structuring Recommendation

The recommended filing position is: **“Specialty Physical Therapy and Mobility Recovery Center in Phuket”** under **2.2.1.1 Specialty medical center**, with the business narrative built around shortage-area rehabilitation medicine rather than around wellness lodging or retirement care. BOI expressly lists physical therapy under the shortage areas for 2.2.1.1 and requires: an appropriate human-resource recruitment plan, board-approved tools and equipment, the relevant health-establishment license before use of CIT incentives and before the full-operation deadline, compliance with Ministry of Public Health professional standards, and consideration of service distribution and patient access. BOI also states that tools and equipment may be used with patients outside the promoted project, but the related revenue must be excluded from CIT-privileged revenue. citeturn3view0

This is materially better than submitting the project primarily as **2.2.2.2 Health rehabilitation center**, because that route gives you the overnight-treatment concept but places the activity in **Group B**, which means **no CIT exemption** for the activity itself. BOI’s 2.2.2.2 conditions are still useful as a design reference for the overnight wing—especially the requirements around medical technology and continuous rehab programs including overnight treatment—but they are not the best entry point if the objective is to maximize incentives. citeturn3view0turn9view2

There is also an important **classification trap** to avoid. BOI activity **2.2.2.1 Senior/dependent care center** requires **Thai shareholders of not less than 51%**, must be a center under the Health Business Establishments Act, must have more than 31 beds for overnight stays, and must provide overnight care plus rehabilitation activities. That means if the project is marketed or operated primarily as an elder-care residence with rehabilitation, it risks being pulled away from the intended specialty-medical-center framing. BOI’s separate **2.2.1.4 Hospital** category also exists for projects with **more than 31 beds** for admitted patients, with A4 rather than A2 incentives. citeturn3view0turn9view2

The practical result is straightforward: if you want the best BOI positioning, the overnight component should be presented as **supportive accommodation for intensive rehabilitation cycles**, not as the main business. In the business plan, the “hero sentence” should be that the company delivers **specialized physical therapy, functional restoration, and mobility-recovery programs under Swiss operating standards**, with short-stay supervised accommodation only where clinically necessary for continuity of care. That structure matches the logic of 2.2.1.1 far better than a rehabilitation-resort or elderly-care narrative. citeturn3view0turn9view1turn9view2

The national aging trend is clear, and Phuket’s own older-person base is already material. ## Regulatory and Licensing Strategy

The core professional rule is non-negotiable: in Thailand, a person who is not a practitioner of physical therapy may not practice physical therapy, and registration and licensing are controlled by the Physical Therapy Council. The Physical Therapy Act also states that license applicants must be members of the council, possess the necessary qualifications, and pass the council’s exam. For foreign graduates, the PT Council’s published procedure requires council membership, a degree from a foreign institute certified by the council, passing the proficiency test, and—if the applicant is a non-Thai citizen—evidence of a physical-therapy license in the foreign country. citeturn12view0turn34view0

That has a direct business implication for your Swiss partner and any foreign clinical staff. They may absolutely be central to ownership, management, training, protocol design, quality systems, and brand positioning under BOI non-tax incentives for skilled workers and experts, but they should **not** be assumed to deliver hands-on physical therapy to patients unless they have gone through the Thai licensing route or fall within the narrow consultant/specialist/lecturer pathway recognized by the PT Act. The Act allows a consultant, specialist, or lecturer licensed in another country to practice with approval of the Board, but that is not the same as a general operating assumption for foreign clinicians. citeturn28view0turn12view0turn34view0

For the overnight component, the safest legal stance is to say that the **final license class must be confirmed early with the Provincial Public Health Office and the Department of Health Service Support**, because the boundary between a specialty medical center, a healthcare establishment with overnight admitted patients, and an elderly/dependent-care establishment matters a great deal. The Department of Health Service Support’s licensing manual for elderly/dependent-care establishments defines that business as care, health promotion, rehabilitation, or palliative support for elderly or dependent persons through day activities, assistance in daily living, or provision of residential premises—but specifically **excepts operations conducted in healthcare facilities under the law on healthcare facilities**. That same manual also confirms electronic filing through the ESTA portal for that license type. citeturn35view0turn36view1

The right strategic response is to keep Phase One legally simple. File the BOI project as a specialty medical center; keep the overnight wing modest and explicitly subordinate to clinical rehab; avoid describing the project as a retirement home; and ensure that all BOI-facing documents show the promoted activity as physical therapy first. If BOI or health regulators later indicate that the overnight scope pushes you into a different license class, you can still preserve value by carving that component into a second legal entity or by sequencing it after the promoted rehab core is operational. citeturn3view0turn35view0

## Clinical Service Model and Operations

The clinical model should be organized around **rehabilitation pathways**, not around room nights. The promoted core should include initial evaluation, treatment planning, supervised therapy sessions, intensive day-rehabilitation packages, discharge planning, home-program design, tele-follow-up, and documented outcomes measurement. The first patient cohorts should be: orthopedic post-surgical recovery; stroke and neurological recovery; geriatric balance, gait, and fall-risk programs; chronic neck/back/spine rehabilitation; and medically supervised return-to-function programs for long-stay visitors living in Phuket. This service mix aligns with the shortage-area designation for physical therapy and reduces the risk that the project is viewed as a hotel or elder residence. citeturn3view0

The human-resource plan submitted to BOI should be unusually concrete. It should specify the year-one staffing build, the Thai-license status required for treating clinicians, the foreign-expert roles, the referral plan, and the training model. A good first-phase template is: Thai-licensed physical therapists as the primary treating workforce; registered nurses for overnight care; one rehabilitation-medicine physician or other duly licensed medical lead depending on the final facility-license class; patient coordinators for foreign cases; and Swiss operating personnel focused on governance, quality assurance, protocol development, staff training, and international patient acquisition. This is the type of “appropriate human resource recruitment plan” BOI explicitly asks for under 2.2.1.1. citeturn3view0turn12view0turn34view0

The equipment plan should be written like a regulatory annex, not like a marketing brochure. BOI requires board-approved tools and equipment for the specialty center, and the Physical Therapy Council’s rules page specifically lists the governing legal framework for physical-therapy instruments, including the original Ministry notice and a **2025 amendment**. Your schedule should therefore cross-map every proposed device to a rehabilitation function and to the relevant professional/legal category. A robust first-phase list would usually include diagnostic and assessment tools, gait and balance systems, rehab gym equipment, electrotherapy and modality equipment, shockwave/laser/ultrasound if clinically justified, traction where appropriate, ADL-training equipment, and digital documentation/outcomes systems. Local competitors also show that advanced rehabilitation equipment is already part of the Phuket standard of care, so your equipment schedule has to be clinically serious. citeturn3view0turn10search1turn22search5turn25search9

The overnight wing should be operationally defined as a **continuity-of-care unit**. In the business plan, describe it as short-stay supervised accommodation for patients on intensive rehab pathways who need closely sequenced sessions, nursing observation, caregiver training, or postoperative support—not as general recuperation lodging. That distinction helps on both BOI classification and revenue segregation. It also creates a cleaner story for physician referrals from Phuket hospitals and for partnerships with insurers or self-pay medical travelers. citeturn3view0turn25search1turn25search2

## Financial Architecture and BOI Tax Design

The finance model should be built on **three ledgers**, even if you initially operate through one company: **promoted rehab revenue**, **non-promoted accommodation/hospitality revenue**, and **shared-cost allocations**. BOI’s guide is explicit that tools and equipment may be used with service recipients outside the promoted project, but revenue from those activities must not be mixed into the revenue eligible for CIT incentives. In practical terms, that means separate service codes, separate invoices, separate management accounts, and a conservative cost-allocation methodology prepared from day one. citeturn3view0

The tax benefit should be modeled conservatively. Under the BOI guide, **A2** gives **8 years** of CIT exemption, but **A2–A4 exemptions are capped at 100% of the project’s investment as determined by BOI**. Thailand’s standard CIT rate is **20%**. So if, for illustration, BOI recognized **80 million baht** of promoted project investment, the maximum amount of profit shielded by the cap would be **80 million baht**, and the maximum headline tax saving at a 20% rate would be about **16 million baht**, unless additional competitiveness-enhancement incentives were later earned. That is still meaningful, but it is very different from assuming eight uncapped years of tax-free profit. citeturn28view0turn20search0

There is, however, an upside lever. BOI’s competitiveness-enhancement measures allow additional incentive periods based on qualifying expenditures such as R&D, advanced technology training, internships, and supplier development. BOI also states that where **R&D spending reaches at least 1% of total sales in the first three years or at least 200 million baht, whichever is lower**, the project may obtain CIT exemption **without a cap**, with the total exemption period extendable up to **13 years** for A2 projects. For a rehabilitation center, that suggests a possible Phase-Two strategy: formalize outcomes research, build protocol-development work with Thai academic institutions, and document advanced training expenditures from the outset. citeturn28view0turn9view3

Capital budgeting should reflect the regulatory objective, not just the operator’s aesthetic preference. If the project wants to maximize the value of the A2 promotion, it should ensure that the promoted entity directly owns or contracts for as much of the genuinely clinical rehab infrastructure as BOI will recognize. If the company instead puts too much spend into luxury room product, general hospitality fit-out, or non-promoted wellness services, the economic value of the CIT exemption will shrink even if the certificate is approved. In other words: build the investment stack so that the **promoted medical core is the capex core**. citeturn28view0turn3view0

## Implementation Roadmap

The BOI process itself is manageable if the filing is disciplined. BOI’s published procedure says eligible applicants submit through the e-Investment Promotion system; BOI schedules project presentation within **10 working days** of submission; projects with investment value up to **200 million baht** are considered within **40 working days** after complete documents; BOI notifies the resolution within **7 days** of certification; applicants must accept the promotion within **1 month**; and the promotion-certificate application must be filed within **6 months**, after which BOI issues the certificate within **10 working days** of receiving complete documents. BOI also lists the typical application-file components, including financial statements if any, business history and details, pictures or catalogues, process documentation, machinery documentation, and evidence of compliance with special conditions. ## Open Questions and Limitations

The business case is strong, but several points still need formal confirmation before the filing is locked. The largest one is the **final facility-license classification** for the overnight wing. The official sources clearly show that BOI distinguishes specialty medical centers, hospitals, senior/dependent care centers, and health rehabilitation centers, and the HSS elderly-care licensing manual distinguishes elderly/dependent-care establishments from healthcare facilities under the healthcare-facilities law. But the exact line your planned accommodation model will cross depends on bed count, admission process, nursing scope, patient type, and the final clinical protocols. citeturn3view0turn35view0turn36view1

The second unresolved point is the **intended clinical role of foreign professionals**. BOI non-tax incentives support the import of skilled workers and experts, but the PT Act separately controls who may actually practice physical therapy in Thailand. If the Swiss side expects direct patient treatment rights from day one, that expectation needs to be reworked around Thai licensing or narrowly tailored approvals. citeturn28view0turn12view0turn34view0

The third is the **economic ceiling created by the A2 cap**. If the promoted investment base is small and the project is highly profitable, the CIT cap may be exhausted well before year eight. The business plan should therefore be presented to investors with the tax holiday modeled as a capped benefit, with a separate upside case for competitiveness-enhancement incentives if the company genuinely intends to spend on R&D and advanced training. citeturn28view0turn20search0

On balance, the best plan is to **file and build as a specialty physical therapy center first**, keep the overnight wing clinically necessary and economically secondary, and treat **2.2.2.2** as either a fallback classification or a second-entity expansion path—not as the front-door identity of the project. That structure best protects foreign-investor flexibility, preserves the strongest available BOI tax position, and fits the regulatory evidence most cleanly. citeturn3view0turn28view0
